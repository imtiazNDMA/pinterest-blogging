# Guideline: Conversion Copywriting

How to turn a reader into a click/buyer **without being sleazy** — trust converts better than pressure, and
it protects the affiliate accounts. Used by content-writer + monetization-strategist; checked by editor-qa.
All affiliate rules in `guardrails/affiliate-compliance.md` are mandatory (no prices, disclosure above first link).

## The conversion mindset
- **Help the reader decide, don't sell.** The goal is the *right* pick for *their* situation. Honesty (naming a
  downside, saying "skip this if…") raises conversion because it earns trust.
- **Reduce friction:** the reader should know *what to get and why* in seconds. Confusion = no click.
- **Match the buying stage:** informational readers need education first; buyer-intent readers want a fast confident pick.

## Recommendation framing that converts
1. **Lead with the verdict, then justify:** "Best overall: the X — because [specific reason for this reader]."
2. **Selection criteria first** (also an anti-slop + E-E-A-T move): state how you judged, then the picks. Readers
   trust a ranking with visible reasoning.
3. **"Best for…" segmentation** beats one winner: Best overall / Best for tiny spaces / Best budget pick /
   Best if you forget to water. Everyone finds *their* pick → more clicks.
   - **Each segment must be a genuinely DISTINCT product** — a different category, form factor, or
     material that actually suits that use case. Two near-identical items relabeled (e.g. two floor grow
     boxes as "Best overall" and "Best for small spaces") is a fail: it gives the reader no real choice
     and reads as filler. If two picks would be the same product, cut one or replace it with a true
     alternative (e.g. a rail planter vs. a floor box vs. a stacked/vertical tower).
4. **Concrete benefit, not adjectives:** not "high quality and durable" → "the thick walls don't crack in
   frost, so it survives winter on an exposed balcony."
5. **Name the trade-off** for each pick. "The catch: it's heavy once filled — fine on a balcony floor, not a railing."
6. **Qualitative value only** — "a worthwhile splurge that lasts years" / "the budget-friendly choice". **No prices/numbers** (Amazon ToS).

## CTAs (calls to action)
- **Specific + low-pressure:** "Check current options on Amazon" / "See the [product] →". Avoid "BUY NOW!!".
- **Placement:** one near each pick (after the reasoning), and one in a summary/comparison table. Don't stack CTAs.
- **Descriptive anchor text** (also SEO): "self-watering rail planter", not "click here".
- **Disclosure above the first affiliate link** — always.

## Trust & persuasion levers (use honestly)
- **Comparison table** (features/best-for, NOT prices) — lets readers self-select fast.
- **Quick-pick box** up top for impatient buyers; detail below for researchers.
- **Specificity = credibility:** sizes, materials, varieties, who it suits.
- **Pre-empt objections:** "Worried about drainage? All three have…".
- **Internal links** to related buyer + how-to posts (keeps them on-site, more chances to convert).
- **Soft scarcity only if true** — never fake "selling out" (FTC + trust). Prefer evergreen framing.

## Don't
- No prices, "cheapest", discounts, or stock claims. No fabricated reviews, ratings, or "I tested" claims.
- No pressure tactics, fake urgency, or guilt ("support me by clicking"). No CTA stuffing.
- No recommending something just because it pays more — quality fit first (then prefer the higher-rate program when equal).
