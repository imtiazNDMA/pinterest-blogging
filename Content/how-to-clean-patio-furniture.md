To clean patio furniture, rinse off the loose dirt, then wash it with warm water and a few drops of mild dish soap. Scrub gently, rinse, and dry it before the grime bakes back on.

That basic method handles most of the job. The rest is knowing what changes for plastic, metal, wicker, wood, and fabric cushions.

This guide walks each material in order, then covers the part most people skip: protecting and storing the set so you are not scrubbing the same mildew next spring.

It is the upkeep companion to our [patio furniture ideas for tight spaces](/patio-furniture-ideas), so the seating you bought there actually lasts.

## The whole routine in one glance

Short on time? Here is every step of the job, start to finish.

- **Rinse** loose dirt and cobwebs with a hose or a bucket of water.
- **Wash** with warm water and a few drops of mild dish soap on a soft cloth or brush.
- **Match the soap to the material:** gentle on everything, never a wire brush on coated metal or plastic.
- **Rinse fully** so no soap film dries on and attracts more dirt.
- **Dry in the sun** before you cover or store anything, because trapped damp is what grows mildew.
- **Spot-test** any stronger cleaner on a hidden patch first.

## How to clean patio furniture by material

The frame decides what you can scrub with and what will strip the finish. Start gentle every time, then step up only if a stain refuses to budge.

### Plastic and resin furniture

Plastic and resin are the most forgiving things on a patio. Wipe them down with warm water and dish soap, scrub with a soft brush, and rinse.

For stubborn grey film or green algae, add a half cup of white vinegar to a bucket of warm soapy water. Let it sit a couple of minutes before scrubbing.

Skip abrasive pads and scouring powder. They scratch the surface, and those tiny scratches trap dirt that gets harder to clean each season.

To brighten badly faded white resin, a baking soda paste worked over the surface lifts a lot of the dullness without scratching.

### Powder-coated metal, aluminum, and wrought iron

Coated metal cleans like plastic: warm soapy water, a soft cloth, a full rinse, and a dry-off so water spots do not set.

The thing to protect is the coating. A scratch in powder coat or paint is where rust starts, so never take a wire brush or steel wool to a finished surface.

If you find a rust spot on wrought iron or steel, sand just the bare patch lightly and wipe it clean. Touch it up with an outdoor-rated metal primer and paint so it does not spread.

Dry metal fully after washing and after rain. Standing water sitting in a chair joint overnight is what turns a small chip into a rust streak.

### Wicker and rattan, natural or synthetic

Most outdoor wicker today is synthetic resin (all-weather PE) woven over a metal frame, and it cleans easily. Vacuum or brush the weave first to pull grit out of the gaps.

Then wash with warm soapy water and a soft brush, working into the crevices where dust collects. Rinse with a gentle hose stream, not a pressure washer, which can unravel the weave.

Natural rattan and cane need a lighter hand. Use a barely damp cloth, clean fast, and dry it quickly, because natural fibers swell, sag, and split if they soak.

Get into the tight corners of any weave with an old toothbrush. That is where mildew likes to start on wicker, hidden down in the strands.

### Real wood and teak

Wash sealed or painted wood with mild soapy water and a soft brush, going with the grain, then rinse and let it dry fully.

Teak is the low-drama one. Many owners simply let it weather to a silver-grey patina and scrub it once a year with soapy water and a soft brush.

To bring the original honey color back, a dedicated teak cleaner lifts the grey. A coat of teak sealer slows future greying, though plenty of people happily skip both.

Avoid bleach and harsh degreasers on bare wood. They can raise the grain, blotch the color, and dry the timber out so it cracks sooner.

## How do you clean patio cushions and outdoor fabric?

Cushions are where most of the real grime lives, and they reward a little routine. Always check the care tag first, since it tells you whether the cover unzips and whether the fabric can be machine washed.

### Spot clean little messes

For a fresh spill or bird dropping, act fast. Brush off the solid bits, then dab the spot with warm water and a drop of mild soap on a sponge.

Blot from the outside of the stain inward so you do not spread the ring, then rinse the area and let it air-dry.

### Deep clean the whole cushion

For a full wash, mix mild soap into a bucket of warm water and sponge the entire surface so you do not leave a clean patch surrounded by dingy fabric.

Let the solution soak in for several minutes, scrub gently with a soft brush, then rinse thoroughly. Soap left in the foam attracts dirt and can feel sticky later.

Stand the cushions on edge to drip and dry. Quick-dry outdoor foam sheds water far faster than indoor foam, which is why it is worth buying for outdoor seating.

### Remove mildew and mould safely

Mildew shows up as black or grey speckles, usually where a cushion stayed damp under a cover. Brush the dry spots off outdoors first so you are not grinding spores deeper into the weave.

Here is the honest safety nuance on bleach. Solution-dyed acrylic fabric (such as Sunbrella) tolerates a diluted bleach mix per the maker's own care guide, but many other outdoor fabrics fade or weaken with bleach.

So check the care tag, and only use bleach if the fabric maker says it is safe. When in doubt, reach for a fabric-safe mildew remover or a vinegar-and-water solution instead, and spot-test on a hidden corner first.

For acrylic that does allow it, the standard mix is roughly one cup of bleach and a quarter cup of mild soap per gallon of water. Soak about 15 minutes, scrub gently, and rinse fully ([Sunbrella mold and mildew cleaning guide](https://www.sunbrella.com/blog/how-to-clean-mold-and-mildew-from-sunbrella-fabrics)).

Protect the surrounding area when you use bleach, since overspray can discolor nearby fabrics, decking, and plants. Work in the open air, and keep pets off the wet area until it has dried and rinsed.

### Dry cushions completely

This is the step that decides whether the mildew comes back. A cushion that feels dry on top can still hold water in the foam core.

Dry cushions fully in the sun, flipping them so both faces and the edges air out, before you stack, cover, or store them.

## Protect it after cleaning

Clean furniture is the moment to add protection, while the surfaces are dry and bare. A little effort now means less scrubbing all season.

Treat washed, dry fabric with a [fabric protector spray]({{AMAZON_TAG}}#affiliate) that restores water and stain resistance. It helps spills bead up and roll off instead of soaking into the foam.

Coated metal benefits from touch-up paint on any chips, as covered above, so bare spots never get the chance to rust.

Between uses, a breathable, fitted cover keeps rain, pollen, and UV off the whole set. Look for a [weatherproof patio furniture cover]({{AMAZON_TAG}}#affiliate) sized to your pieces, with vents so condensation can escape rather than pool inside.

## Storing cushions and furniture for the off-season

When the season ends, dry storage is what carries a set into next year. Damp is the enemy at every step here.

Store cushions clean and bone-dry, off the ground, somewhere ventilated like a garage shelf, a deck box, or inside. A breathable [cushion storage bag]({{AMAZON_TAG}}#affiliate) keeps dust off while still letting any trace moisture escape, which a sealed bin can trap.

If you build storage into the seating itself, our [DIY patio furniture guide](/diy-patio-furniture) has a lift-top bench plan that hides cushions over winter.

For the furniture frames, bring lightweight pieces indoors where you can, and cover or stack the rest in a sheltered corner. Folding and stacking chairs make this easy, which is one reason they suit small spaces, as our [balcony furniture ideas](/balcony-furniture-ideas) guide explains.

Raise wood and metal legs off a wet slab with feet or a pallet so they never wick up standing water through the cold months.

## How often should you clean patio furniture?

A quick wipe-down every couple of weeks in peak season keeps grime from building into the kind of stain that needs real scrubbing.

Plan one deeper clean at the start of the season and another before you pack everything away. Those two are the ones that matter most.

Cushions get spot-cleaned as messes happen, plus a full wash if they start to look or smell musty. Rinsing off pollen, salt, or bird droppings the day you spot them saves the set from set-in stains later.

Climate changes the math. Damp, shaded, or coastal spots grow mildew faster, so check those setups more often than a dry, sunny balcony.

## Cleaning patio furniture FAQ

### How do I clean plastic patio furniture that has gone grey?

Wash with warm soapy water first. Then tackle the grey film with a half cup of white vinegar in a bucket of warm soapy water, or a baking soda paste rubbed over the surface.

Both lift oxidation and algae without scratching. Skip abrasive scouring pads, which leave micro-scratches that trap dirt and make the next clean harder.

### Can I use a pressure washer on patio furniture?

Be cautious. A pressure washer can strip paint, splinter wood, force water into cushion foam, and unravel wicker weave if you hold it too close.

If you do use one, pick the lowest setting, keep the nozzle well back, and test on a hidden spot first. For most furniture, a hose and a soft brush are safer and plenty effective.

### How do I get mildew out of outdoor cushions?

Brush off the dry speckles outdoors, then check the care tag. For solution-dyed acrylic, a diluted bleach-and-soap solution is the maker-approved fix; for other fabrics, use a fabric-safe mildew remover or vinegar and water and spot-test first.

Scrub gently, rinse thoroughly, and dry the cushions completely in the sun. Storing them damp is what brings mildew straight back.

### How do I clean metal patio furniture without ruining the finish?

Use warm soapy water and a soft cloth, never steel wool or a wire brush on a coated surface. Rinse and dry fully so water spots and rust do not set.

For a rust spot on bare metal, sand just that patch, then prime and paint it with an outdoor-rated product so it does not spread.

### How should I store patio cushions over winter?

Clean and fully dry them first, then keep them off the floor in a ventilated, dry spot such as a deck box, a garage shelf, or indoors.

A breathable storage bag beats a sealed plastic bin, which can trap moisture and grow the mildew you just removed.
