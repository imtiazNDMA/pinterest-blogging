The best privacy planters for a patio do two jobs at once: they hold a tall plant at eye level and they stand as part of the screen themselves.

Get the planter wrong and the plant flops, the box blows over, or the whole row sits too low to hide anything. Most of that comes down to two numbers: how deep the planter is, and how heavy it gets once it holds soil, water, and a tall plant in the wind.

Below is how to choose a planter that actually screens, then six distinct styles by form factor, each with its trade-off named. After that, the pet-safe plants that fill them in fast.

## How do you choose privacy planters for a patio?

Start with the height you actually need to block, not the planter that looks good in the photo. Measure from the slab to the eyeline you want hidden, usually around 5 to 6 feet for a standing neighbor, less for a seated one.

The planter covers the bottom of that height; the plant covers the rest. So a 24-inch planter holding a 4-foot grass screens to roughly 6 feet, which is the math most people skip.

### Depth and weight come first

Tall plants need root room and ballast, or they tip. Look for at least 12 to 16 inches of soil depth for grasses and columnar shrubs, and a wide, flat base that resists toppling.

Here, weight works in your favor. A heavy filled box is what keeps a tall plant upright in a gust, so set the planter in its final spot before you fill it.

### Trough or box versus round

A long rectangular trough lines several plants into one unbroken screen, which is what you want along an open edge. A round planter screens a narrower slice, so it suits a single statement plant or a corner rather than a run.

A privacy planter box with straight sides also butts up flush against the next one, closing the gaps a round pot leaves open.

### Trellis, reservoir, and wheels

Three features earn their keep on a patio screen:

- **A built-in trellis** turns a planter into an instant frame for a climber, so you get screen height on day one while the vine fills in.
- **A self-watering reservoir** wicks moisture up to thirsty roots for days, which matters because a tall plant in a sunny pot drinks fast.
- **Casters or wheels** let you roll a heavy box to chase the sun or clear the slab, instead of dragging 80 pounds of wet soil.

### Material, drainage, and wind

Resin and fiberglass stay light and shrug off frost, the easy default for a tall planter you may need to move. Wood and metal look warmer but add weight and need sealing or rust touch-ups.

Every planter needs drainage holes, or the first hard rain drowns the roots, unless it is a self-watering model with an overflow. And because a tall plant acts like a sail, keep the heaviest planters on your windiest edge.

## How these picks were chosen

Each planter below was judged against the five things that decide whether it screens a view and keeps a tall plant alive, rather than how it photographs.

1. **Screening height.** The planter plus its intended plant has to reach a real eyeline, not stop at floor-level decoration.

3. **Root depth.** Enough soil for grasses, columnar evergreens, or a climber to anchor and feed.
4. **Drainage or reservoir.** Holes plus overflow, or a sealed self-watering tank, so roots never sit in rain.
5. **A reason to exist.** Each pick is a genuinely different form factor, so you choose by how your patio is laid out, not by color.

Match the one that fits your layout and how you garden. You rarely need more than one or two styles to screen a whole edge.

### Best overall living screen: a long rectangular trough

**Who it's for:** anyone screening an open edge or fence line who wants one continuous green wall.

**Why it wins:** a long trough lines up three or four tall plants at even spacing, so they read as a single hedge instead of scattered pots. The straight sides push flush against the next trough, closing the gaps, and the broad base holds tall grasses steady. Look for one with real depth and drainage along the bottom.

**The trade-off:** a full trough is heavy and awkward to shift once planted. Set it exactly where you want the screen before you add soil, because you will not be sliding it later.

## What to plant in a patio privacy planter

The right plant turns a planter into a screen. For dense, fast cover in a pot, lean on tall ornamental grasses, a contained bamboo, columnar evergreens, or a climber up a trellis.

Keep every plant pet-safe if cats or dogs share the patio, since many common screening shrubs are toxic. Check anything new against the [ASPCA toxic and non-toxic plant list](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants) before you plant it.

### Pet-safe screening plants in pots

These are listed by the ASPCA as non-toxic to cats and dogs, which makes them safer patio privacy plants in pots if pets are around:

- **Fountain grass (Pennisetum).** Tall, feathery, and fast to fill a trough. The ASPCA lists it non-toxic to cats and dogs, though the seed heads can be a physical irritant, so trim spent plumes if your pet chews them.
- **True bamboo (Phyllostachys).** Grows tall and leafy for a quick front screen, and the ASPCA lists bamboo (Phyllostachys aurea) as non-toxic. Grow it in a sealed, contained planter, because running types spread aggressively and are hard to remove once they escape a pot.
- **Star jasmine (Trachelospermum jasminoides) and climbing rose (Rosa).** Both train up a trellis planter for a flowering screen, and both are listed non-toxic, though rose thorns can still scratch a curious pet.

A safety note on names: "lucky bamboo" and "heavenly" or "sacred bamboo" (Nandina) are not true bamboo and are toxic, so buy by botanical name, not the label.

### Screening plants to avoid around pets

Several classic privacy shrubs are listed as toxic by the ASPCA, so skip these where pets roam: boxwood, oleander (highly toxic), English ivy, clematis, and privet. Maiden grass (Miscanthus) and arborvitae (Thuja) are not clearly listed by the ASPCA either way, so treat both as unverified and keep pets away from them until you confirm with your vet.

When you are unsure about any plant, check it against the ASPCA list above or ask your vet before it goes on the patio. For fuller planting and care detail, see our [patio garden ideas](/patio-garden-ideas) guide.

## Patio privacy planter FAQ

### How tall should a privacy planter be to screen a patio?

Aim for the planter plus plant to reach the eyeline you want hidden, roughly 5 to 6 feet for a standing neighbor. A planter around 20 to 24 inches tall holding a 3 to 4 foot grass or shrub usually gets you there. Measure from your slab to the sightline first, then split that height between pot and plant.

### What are the best tall planters for privacy on a windy patio?

Pick a wide, heavy-based trough or box and fill it with real soil for ballast, since weight is what holds a top-heavy plant upright. Keep the tallest planters on your most exposed edge, choose bushy plants over thin top-heavy ones, and stand a trellis planter against a wall until its climber anchors it.

### Do privacy planters need a trellis?

Only if you want to grow a climber. A trellis gives you instant screen height and a frame for star jasmine or a climbing rose, which is ideal for a front sightline. For tall grasses, bamboo, or columnar evergreens, a deep trough without a trellis screens just fine on its own.

### Can I move a planted privacy screen later?

A filled trough is heavy and awkward, so plan its spot before you fill it. If you expect to reposition the screen, a rolling planter box on lockable casters is built for it. You push the heavy plant across the slab, then lock it back into the screen line.

### Are privacy planters pet-safe?

The planter is, but the plant is the question. Fountain grass, true bamboo, star jasmine, and climbing rose are listed non-toxic by the ASPCA, while boxwood, oleander, ivy, clematis, and privet are toxic. Confirm any plant against the [ASPCA list](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants) before it shares the patio with a cat or dog.
