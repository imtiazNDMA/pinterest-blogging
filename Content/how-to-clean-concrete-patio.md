Learning how to clean a concrete patio comes down to four moves: clear the slab, pre-treat the stains, wash the surface, then rinse it well.

Do those in order and most patios go from grey and grimy to bright in an afternoon. You do not need a pressure washer to get there, though one makes the job faster.

A cement patio cleans the same way a poured slab does, so the steps below work whether yours is smooth, broom-finished, or laid as pavers.

## The quick version

To clean a concrete patio fast and well, work in this order:

1. **Clear and sweep** every leaf, pot, and bit of furniture off the slab.
2. **Pre-treat stains** with a targeted cleaner and let it dwell.
3. **Wash the surface** with a pressure washer, or with a stiff brush and cleaner.
4. **Rinse thoroughly** so no cleaner dries on the concrete.
5. **Seal it** once a year if you want the clean to last (optional).

## How to clean a concrete patio, step by step

Here is the full routine for a deep clean of the whole slab. The order matters more than the products.

### 1. Clear and sweep

Move the furniture, pots, and rug off the patio so you can reach every inch. Anything left on the slab leaves a clean ghost where it sat.

Sweep the whole surface with a stiff push broom. Get into the corners and along the wall, where leaves and grit pack in and feed future stains.

### 2. Pre-treat the stains

Hit the obvious marks first, while the slab is dry. Grease, mildew patches, and rust each want a targeted cleaner, covered in the stain section below.

Spray or pour your cleaner onto each stain and let it dwell. Ten to fifteen minutes of soak time does more than scrubbing a dry spot ever will.

Keep the dwelling cleaner wet. If it starts drying in the sun, mist it again so it keeps working.

### 3. Wash with a pressure washer

To pressure wash a concrete patio, fit a 25-degree (wide) nozzle and start in a back corner. A narrow tip can etch lines into the surface, so skip the zero-degree nozzle on concrete.

Hold the wand about 12 inches off the slab and keep it moving in steady, overlapping passes. Pausing in one spot leaves a clean halo that shows up the moment everything dries.

Work in the same direction across the whole patio so the finish stays even. Then go back over any stain that is still visible.

### 4. Rinse thoroughly

Rinse from the high side toward your drain or lawn edge so dirty water never runs back over clean concrete. Push the runoff with the broom if your slab is dead level.

Cleaner left to dry on concrete can leave a haze or a sticky film. A full rinse is what makes the slab look genuinely clean rather than just wet.

### 5. Seal it (optional, but it pays off)

A clear concrete sealer is the step that makes this clean last. It blocks oil, slows algae, and helps the next wash go faster.

Let the slab dry fully first, usually a day or two of dry weather. Then follow the product directions and respect the cure time before you walk on it or move furniture back.

## Can you clean a concrete patio without a pressure washer?

Yes. You can clean a concrete patio without a pressure washer using a stiff brush, a bucket of cleaner, and a hose, and the result is just as bright with a bit more elbow work.

Wet the slab first with the hose so the cleaner spreads instead of soaking straight in. Mix your concrete cleaner per its directions in a bucket.

Pour the solution over a section you can reach, then scrub in small circles with the stiff brush. Work one zone at a time so no patch dries before you rinse it.

Rinse each cleaned section before moving on. For a stubborn film, a second pass of cleaner on the still-damp concrete lifts what the first one loosened.

This brush method is also the gentler choice for older or cracked slabs, where high pressure can chew at weak spots and worn joints.

## How to remove stains from a concrete patio

Most of a tired patio is just general grime. The marks that stay after a wash are specific stains, and each one has its own fix.

### Grease and oil

Blot fresh spills, then cover the spot with baking soda or cat litter to pull oil out of the pores. Leave it overnight, sweep it up, and repeat if the litter darkens.

For the remaining shadow, scrub with a strong dish soap or a concrete degreaser and rinse. Old, set oil may need a couple of rounds before it lifts.

### Mold, mildew, and algae

Mildew on a concrete patio shows up as black speckles or a green-grey film, usually in shade or where water sits. Oxygen bleach is the cleaner to reach for first.

Mix the oxygen-bleach solution, spread it over the area, and let it dwell 15 to 20 minutes. Scrub with the stiff brush, then rinse well.

Oxygen bleach is easier on nearby plants and colored concrete than chlorine bleach ([Concrete Network](https://www.concretenetwork.com/concrete/cleaning_concrete/bleach.html)). If you do use diluted chlorine bleach, pre-water the surrounding plants, never mix it with other cleaners, and rinse everything thoroughly afterward.

### Rust

Light rust from a metal chair foot or planter often lifts with white vinegar. Pour it on, give it 10 minutes, and scrub.

For deeper rust, use a rust remover made for concrete and follow its directions. Skip a metal wire brush, since stray bristles leave fresh rust specks behind.

### Leaf and tannin stains

Wet leaves left on concrete bleed brown tannin shadows, the same way they stain a deck. Oxygen bleach handles these well.

Apply it, let it sit, scrub, and rinse. The fix that actually prevents them is sweeping leaves off promptly in autumn rather than letting them mat down and soak.

### Efflorescence (the chalky white film)

That powdery white bloom is efflorescence, mineral salt drawn to the surface as the slab dries. Start dry, because adding water alone just redissolves the salt and moves it.

Brush off as much as you can with a dry stiff brush first ([Western Interlock](https://westerninterlock.com/how-to-remove-efflorescence/)). For what remains, pre-wet the concrete, then apply diluted white vinegar (about one part vinegar to two parts water) or a dedicated efflorescence cleaner, scrub, and rinse.

Keep any pressure washing low and wide here, since too much force opens the pores and invites the bloom right back.

## How often should you deep clean a patio?

A quick sweep and hose-down every week or two keeps grime from building into stains. That light habit is what saves you from the hard scrub later.

Plan a full deep clean once or twice a year, typically in spring to undo winter and again in fall before the leaves pile up. Shady, damp corners that grow mildew may want a spot treatment more often.

## Concrete patio cleaning FAQ

### What is the best thing to clean a concrete patio with?

For everyday grime, a concentrated concrete or patio cleaner with a stiff brush does the job. For mold, mildew, or green algae, oxygen bleach is the best first choice because it lifts organic stains without harming plants or fading colored concrete.

Match the cleaner to the stain rather than reaching for one harsh product. Grease wants a degreaser, rust wants an acid-based remover, and efflorescence wants diluted vinegar.

### How do I clean a concrete patio without killing my plants?

Use oxygen bleach instead of chlorine bleach near beds and pots, since it is gentler on greenery. Water the surrounding plants with plain water before you start and again after you rinse, which dilutes any runoff.

Sweep cleaner toward a drain rather than into planting beds where you can. Never mix cleaners, and keep pets and kids off the slab until it has been fully rinsed and dried.

### Will a pressure washer damage my concrete patio?

It can if you use too narrow a nozzle or hold the wand too close. Stick to a 25-degree wide tip, keep it roughly 12 inches off the surface, and keep it moving in steady passes.

On older or cracked slabs, drop to the brush-and-cleaner method instead. High pressure can erode weak spots, flake the surface, and blow out crumbling joints.

### How do I clean patio pavers without washing out the joint sand?

Clean patio pavers the same way you clean a poured slab, with one tweak: keep the pressure lower and the nozzle moving so you do not blast sand out of the joints. A stiff brush with cleaner is the safest method on sanded pavers.

If you do lose joint sand, top it up and sweep fresh sand back into the gaps once the surface is dry.

### How do I get black spots and mildew off a patio?

Those black speckles are mildew or algae rather than dirt, so plain washing rarely shifts them. Apply oxygen bleach, let it dwell 15 to 20 minutes, scrub with a stiff brush, and rinse.

For a slab that re-spots every season, improve drainage and airflow where water sits, then seal the concrete to slow regrowth.
