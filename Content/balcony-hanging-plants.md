The best balcony hanging plants are trailing varieties matched to your light, then hung in a way your railing and your lease can actually handle.

Get both right and a bare rail turns into a curtain of green and flower in a single season.

This guide sorts trailing plants for balcony spaces by light, flags which ones are toxic to pets, and shows how to hang them with no drilling.

Pick your light, grab two or three names, and skip the trial-and-error most beginners pay for twice.

## The short version

Before you buy a single basket, line up these four calls.

1. **Count your real sun hours.** Six or more is full sun, three to six is part shade, under three is low light. This decides your whole plant list.
2. **Plan for fast drying.** A hanging basket dries faster than any pot, because wind hits it on every side. Pick forgiving plants or a self-watering basket.
3. **Hang it without drilling.** Over-rail hooks, clamp-on brackets, freestanding arches, and macrame on an existing hook all skip the drill and the deposit fight.
4. **Check pets first.** Many trailing favorites are toxic to cats and dogs. Stick to the ASPCA-verified list below if animals share the space.

Nail those four and almost everything that follows thrives. The picks are grouped by exactly these constraints.

## How to choose hanging plants that actually thrive up high

Most hanging baskets fail for one boring reason: the plant never matched the spot.

A sun-lover in a shady corner stretches thin and stops blooming. A moisture-lover in a windy basket crisps by noon.

So the order matters. Read your light first, then your wind, then your watering habit, and only then pick a plant.

A good trailing plant for a balcony also needs to spill. You want a spiller that cascades over the rim, not an upright plant that just sits there.

For the wider planning method, layout, and zoning, the [balcony garden ideas hub](/balcony-garden-ideas) covers the whole space. This guide stays focused on what hangs and trails.

## Best balcony hanging plants for full sun (6+ hours)

You step onto a south or west balcony at noon and the floor is hot underfoot. That heat unlocks the showiest baskets on this list.

**Trailing petunias and calibrachoa** (Million Bells) pour over a basket in non-stop color from spring to frost. Calibrachoa is self-cleaning, so you skip the deadheading, and both want a 10 to 12 inch basket.

Nasturtiums trail and bloom in sun, and the ornamental kind is edible down to the peppery flowers. They thrive on a little neglect and poor soil.

Bacopa spills in a sheet of tiny white or blue flowers and pairs well as a filler around bolder blooms.

For a hot, dry rail you forget to water, a trailing succulent earns its hook. Burro's tail (Sedum morganianum) cascades in plump blue-green beads and shrugs off missed waterings.

String of pearls is the trendy sun trailer everyone pins, but it is toxic to cats and dogs. On a pet balcony, reach for burro's tail instead (more on that below).

## Best balcony hanging plants for shade or a north-facing rail

A north-facing balcony feels like a disadvantage until a few hanging ferns turn it into a cool, leafy retreat.

These are your best hanging plants for balcony shade, where direct sun is scarce but bright, indirect light is steady.

Fuchsias dangle jewel-toned blooms on just two to four hours of soft sun, which makes them the classic shade-basket flower. The ASPCA lists fuchsia as non-toxic to cats and dogs.

A Boston fern is the lush, old-school hanging fern, happy in bright indirect light and steady moisture. It is also pet-safe, covered below.

Spider plants arch out a fountain of striped leaves and dangling plantlets, and they forgive almost any beginner mistake.

Swedish ivy trails glossy, scalloped foliage fast in part shade and roots from cuttings in water, so one basket soon becomes three.

Skip the obvious shade trailers that happen to be toxic. Pothos, philodendron, and English ivy all trail beautifully but are toxic to cats and dogs, so keep them off a pet balcony.

## Pet-safe hanging plants (ASPCA-verified)

If a cat or dog shares your balcony, the trailing-plant list narrows hard, because several of the most-pinned hanging plants are toxic.

Every plant in this section is listed as non-toxic to dogs and cats by the [ASPCA toxic and non-toxic plant database](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants).

For foliage baskets, a spider plant, a Boston fern, or Swedish ivy all trail or arch and stay safe around animals.

For flowers, trailing petunias, fuchsia, and ornamental nasturtiums give you cascading color without the worry.

For a drought-proof succulent trailer, burro's tail is the pet-safe swap for string of pearls.

Now the ones to keep off a pet balcony. Pothos, philodendron, English ivy, string of pearls, and asparagus fern are all listed as toxic to cats and dogs by the ASPCA.

String of hearts gets pinned as pet-safe, but the listings conflict, so treat it as unconfirmed and keep it out of reach if you grow it. When in doubt, check any new plant against the ASPCA list before it goes on the rail.

## How do you hang plants on a balcony without drilling?

Most rental leases forbid drilling, and concrete balcony slabs fight the drill anyway. The good news is you rarely need one.

Here are the renter-friendly ways to hang a basket, from easiest to most involved.

1. **Over-rail hooks.** An S-hook or a purpose-made over-rail hook drapes over the top rail and holds a basket on the outside edge. It is the fastest fix and uses zero floor space.
2. **Clamp-on rail brackets.** A screw-clamp bracket grips the railing and gives you a swing-out arm for a heavier basket. Tighten it down so wind cannot work it loose.
3. **Freestanding plant stands and arches.** A weighted arch or a tall shepherd's-hook stand carries hanging baskets with no fixings at all, so it lifts out clean on moving day.
4. **Tension rods and existing hooks.** A tension rod wedged across a recess, or the ceiling hook a previous tenant left, both take a lightweight macrame hanger.
5. **Macrame on the balcony above.** If a slab or beam overhangs your space, a macrame hanger on an existing hook drops a basket at eye level without touching your own ceiling.

Adhesive hooks work only for lightweight pots, and they fail fast outdoors as a wet basket gains weight, so trust them only for the smallest planters.

## Pick baskets and pots that hold up outside

The basket matters almost as much as the plant, because it decides how fast the soil dries and how much the wind catches it.

Coco-lined wire baskets look the most natural and drain freely, but they dry the fastest, so they suit thirsty plants you water daily.

Solid plastic or resin baskets hold moisture far longer and weigh less, which is kinder on an over-rail hook and a high balcony.

A self-watering hanging basket has a small reservoir in the base that stretches the gap between waterings on a hot, exposed rail. It is the easiest path if you travel or forget.

Size up where you can. A 12-inch basket holds enough soil to ride out a missed watering, while an 8-inch basket on a sunny rail can cook by lunchtime.

## Care for wind and fast-drying baskets

A hanging basket is the thirstiest container on your balcony, because air moves around all six sides and pulls moisture out fast.

Plan to water daily in summer, and twice on a hot, windy day. Soak until water runs from the base rather than splashing the surface.

To slow the drying, line a coco basket with a sheet of plastic and poke a few holes for drainage. It buys you real slack between waterings.

Wind is the other balcony force. Choose flexible trailers that bend instead of snapping, and check that every hook and clamp is tight before a storm.

Bring baskets down or move them against a wall when a gale is forecast, since a swinging basket can crack a hook or scrape the rail. ## Balcony hanging plants FAQ

### What are the best trailing plants for a balcony in full sun?

Trailing petunias and calibrachoa bloom non-stop in a sunny basket, nasturtiums add edible flowers, and burro's tail is a drought-proof succulent for a rail you forget to water.

All want a 10 to 12 inch basket and six or more hours of direct sun.

### How do you hang plants on a balcony without drilling?

Use over-rail hooks or a clamp-on rail bracket to hang a basket on the railing. A freestanding arch or shepherd's hook needs zero fixings, and a macrame hanger works on an existing overhead hook.

Save adhesive hooks for only the lightest pots, since a wet basket quickly outweighs them.

### Are hanging plants safe for cats and dogs?

Some are. Spider plants, Boston ferns, Swedish ivy, fuchsia, trailing petunias, and burro's tail are all listed as non-toxic to dogs and cats by the ASPCA.

Avoid pothos, philodendron, English ivy, string of pearls, and asparagus fern, which the ASPCA lists as toxic. Always confirm a new plant on the ASPCA list first.

### How often should I water hanging baskets on a balcony?

Daily in summer, and sometimes twice on a hot or windy day, because baskets dry faster than any other container.

Larger baskets, plastic over coco liners, and a self-watering reservoir all stretch the time between waterings.

### What hangs well on a shady, north-facing balcony?

Fuchsia for dangling flowers, plus Boston fern, spider plant, and Swedish ivy for trailing foliage, all thrive in bright indirect light with little or no direct sun.

Treat shade as a different palette built around leaf shape rather than a dead end.

## Hang one basket this weekend

You do not need a full curtain of greenery on day one. Count your sun hours, pick two trailing plants from the section that matches your light, and hang one basket with a no-drill bracket.
