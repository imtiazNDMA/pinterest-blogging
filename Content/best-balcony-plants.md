The best balcony plants are the ones that match your light, your wind, and how often you actually remember to water.

There is no universal top-ten. A plant that thrives on a baking south rail will sulk on a shady north one.

So this guide sorts plants by the real constraint you are working with, with pot sizes and sun hours for each.

Pick your situation, grab two or three names, and you will skip the expensive trial-and-error most beginners pay for.

## The short version

Before you buy anything, match the plant to your spot:

1. **Count your real sun hours.** Six or more is full sun, three to six is part shade, under three is low light. This decides most of your list.
2. **Size the pot up.** Bigger pots hold more soil, dry slower, and forgive a missed watering. Most balcony plants want 10 to 12 inches; fruiting veg wants 14-plus.
3. **Respect the wind.** A high or corner balcony funnels gusts that snap tall stems and parch leaves. Pick low, flexible plants for exposed edges.
4. **Plan the watering.** A balcony has no ground moisture, so pots dry fast. Choose forgiving plants or self-watering pots if you travel.

Get those four right and almost everything below thrives. The picks that follow are grouped by exactly these constraints.

## How to choose balcony plants that actually thrive

Most balcony plants die for one of four reasons, and none of them is bad luck.

Wrong light is the big one. A sun-lover in a shady corner stretches thin and never blooms.

Too-small pots are the second. A 6-inch pot on a hot rail cooks the roots and dries by noon.

Wind is the quiet killer on upper floors. It shreds broad leaves and tips top-heavy pots.

And inconsistent watering finishes the job, because there is no ground to buffer a pot the way a garden bed does.

So before you fall for a pretty photo, answer three questions: your direct sun hours, your wind exposure, and how often you will realistically water. For the full planning walk-through, the [balcony garden ideas hub](/balcony-garden-ideas) covers layout, light, and zoning the space.

## The best balcony plants for full sun (6+ hours)

You step onto a south or west balcony at noon and the floor is hot underfoot. That heat is a gift, because it unlocks the showiest plants on this whole list.

**Petunias and calibrachoa** (Million Bells) bloom non-stop from spring to frost in a 10 to 12 inch pot or hanging basket. Calibrachoa is self-cleaning, so you skip the deadheading.

Zinnias give you cut-flower color from a single sowing. Give them an 8 to 10 inch pot and they bloom in roughly 60 days from seed.

Lavender loves the heat and a gritty, fast-draining mix in a 12-inch pot. Note that lavender is toxic to cats and dogs, so see the pet-safe section if animals share the space.

Geraniums (Pelargonium) shrug off sun and dry spells in an 8 to 10 inch pot. They are also toxic to pets, so keep them out of reach.

For edibles in full sun, a compact tomato like 'Tumbling Tom' fruits happily in a hanging basket or 5-gallon pot. Sun-loving herbs go here too, covered below.

## Best plants for a balcony in shade or facing north

A north-facing balcony feels like a disadvantage until you realize foliage plants make it look like a cool, leafy retreat.

These are your best plants for balcony shade, where direct sun is scarce but bright, indirect light is steady.

Fuchsias bloom in dangling jewel tones with just two to four hours of soft sun, ideal for a hanging basket on a sheltered north rail.

Impatiens carpet a shady 8 to 10 inch pot in color all season and ask for almost nothing but steady moisture.

Coleus earns its spot on leaf color alone, from lime to burgundy, thriving in part shade in a 10-inch pot.

Heuchera (coral bells) holds ruffled foliage from spring to frost and tolerates a north aspect well. Ferns work too; a Boston fern is a lush, pet-safe choice for low light, covered below.

Begonias bloom generously in shade, but note they are toxic to pets, so place them where animals cannot reach.

## The toughest plants for a windy, exposed balcony

Up high, the wind never really stops. It is the difference between a balcony that fills in and one where every tall stem ends up snapped.

The trick is to grow low and flexible, with plants that bend instead of break.

Ornamental grasses like compact fountain grass sway with the gusts and never snap. A 12-inch pot anchors them against tipping.

Sedum and other low succulents store water in fleshy leaves, so wind and drought barely touch them. They want full sun and a shallow, wide pot.

Compact evergreens such as dwarf pittosporum or a small boxwood give you a sturdy, year-round backbone that wind cannot ruffle.

Keep every pot on an exposed edge low and heavy. A wide base and dense potting mix stop the whole thing toppling on a breezy afternoon.

## Low-maintenance balcony plants for forgetful waterers

Some weeks you will forget to water for three days straight. These low maintenance balcony plants forgive you.

They share one trait: they store water or tolerate drought, so a missed watering is a hiccup rather than a death sentence.

Succulents and sedum lead the list. In a shallow terracotta pot with gritty mix, they would rather be left alone than fussed over.

Lavender and rosemary both prefer their soil on the dry side, so neglect suits them in a 10 to 12 inch pot.

Calibrachoa and trailing petunias bloom for months with little more than regular water and full sun. They are about as close to plant-and-forget color as a balcony gets.

If even that feels like a stretch, move your thirstier pots into self-watering planters. A built-in reservoir stretches the gap between waterings, and our guide to the [best self-watering planters for a balcony](/best-self-watering-planters-balcony) compares the styles that work in tight spaces.

## Herbs you will actually cook with

A pot of herbs by the door is the balcony plant most likely to earn its keep, because you snip from it every week.

Most kitchen herbs want the same thing: full sun and a pot that drains.

Basil thrives in an 8 to 10 inch pot with six-plus hours of sun. Pinch the tips often and it bushes out instead of bolting.

Rosemary and thyme both like it sunny and slightly dry, happy in a 10-inch pot with gritty mix. They are also pet-safe, which the section below confirms.

Mint grows fast and forgiving in part shade, but keep it in its own pot. Its roots will strangle anything you plant beside it.

Plant herbs in a light, fast-draining potting mix rather than dense garden soil, which compacts and waterlogs roots in a container.

## Trailing and railing plants for the edge

The railing is the most underused real estate on any balcony, and trailing plants are how you fill it without losing floor space.

Hook a trough over the rail, plant a spiller, and the greenery cascades down the outside where it softens the whole view.

Trailing petunias and calibrachoa pour over a rail planter in a sheet of color all summer in full sun.

Bacopa and lobelia trail in white and blue for a part-shade rail, staying neat without much fuss.

Ivy-leaf geraniums spill beautifully in sun, though they are toxic to pets, so save them for an animal-free balcony.

Nasturtiums trail and bloom in sun, and the ornamental kind is pet-safe and edible. For a deeper menu of blooms by color and season, see our roundup of the [best balcony flowers](/balcony-flowers).

## Pet-safe balcony plants (ASPCA-verified)

If a cat or dog shares your balcony, the plant list narrows, because several balcony favorites are toxic.

Every plant below is listed as non-toxic to dogs and cats by the [ASPCA toxic and non-toxic plant database](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants).

For herbs, basil, rosemary, and thyme are all pet-safe and earn a spot in any sunny pot.

For flowers, petunias, zinnias, sunflowers, snapdragons, and ornamental nasturtiums are non-toxic and give you full-sun color without the worry.

For foliage, a spider plant or a Boston fern brings lush greenery to a shadier corner and is safe around pets.

Several common balcony plants are toxic, so do not assume. Geraniums, lavender, and begonias are all listed as toxic to dogs and cats by the ASPCA.

True marigolds (Tagetes) are flagged as toxic by the Pet Poison Helpline, so keep them off a pet balcony. Note that the ASPCA "garden marigold" entry is actually Calendula, a different, non-toxic plant.

Lilies are the worst risk for cats, where even a small amount can cause kidney failure. Always confirm the botanical name before you buy.

Always check a new plant against the ASPCA list before you bring it home, and keep anything questionable out of reach. For more on a pet-friendly setup, our [apartment balcony garden guide](/apartment-balcony-garden) covers renter-safe, animal-safe arrangements.

## Common balcony plant mistakes to skip

Most balcony plant failures trace back to the same few habits. Dodge these and your survival rate climbs fast.

Buying before you measure light is the costliest. Watch the spot for a day first, then shop to the sun hours you actually counted.

Pots that are too small dry out and stunt roots. When in doubt, size up; a 12-inch pot forgives far more than an 8-inch one.

Dense garden soil suffocates container roots. Use a light, fast-draining potting mix every time.

Ignoring wind on an upper floor snaps stems and tips pots. Keep exposed-edge plants low, flexible, and heavily anchored. For tight layouts that protect plants and still leave room to sit, the [small balcony garden guide](/small-balcony-garden) walks through it.

## Balcony plants FAQ

### What is the easiest plant to grow on a balcony?

Succulents and calibrachoa are the most forgiving. Succulents tolerate missed waterings in a shallow, sunny pot, and calibrachoa blooms for months in full sun with little fuss.

Both suit a beginner balcony better than thirsty, high-maintenance choices.

### What can I grow on a north-facing balcony with no direct sun?

Foliage and shade-tolerant bloomers. Coleus, heuchera, ferns, impatiens, and fuchsias all thrive in bright, indirect light with two to four hours of soft sun or less.

Treat shade as a different palette built around leaf shape and color rather than a dead end.

### How big should balcony plant pots be?

Most flowers and herbs want a 10 to 12 inch pot, and fruiting vegetables want 14 inches or more with real depth.

Bigger pots hold more soil, dry out slower, and forgive missed waterings, which matters a lot on a hot, exposed balcony.

### Which balcony plants are safe for cats and dogs?

Basil, rosemary, thyme, petunias, zinnias, sunflowers, snapdragons, spider plants, and Boston ferns are all listed as non-toxic by the ASPCA.

Avoid geraniums, lavender, begonias, true marigolds, and especially lilies, which are highly toxic to cats. Always confirm a new plant on the ASPCA list first.

### Can balcony plants survive strong wind?

Yes, if you choose for it. Low, flexible plants like ornamental grasses, sedum, and compact evergreens handle gusts that snap taller, top-heavy plants.

Keep exposed pots low and heavily weighted so wind cannot tip them.

## Pick two plants and start this weekend

You do not need a full balcony jungle on day one. Count your sun hours, then choose two plants from the section that matches your light and wind.

Pot them up in a generous container with fresh mix, and let one success teach you the next.
